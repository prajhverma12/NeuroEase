Neuro Divergence

Neurodivergence refers to a range of conditions that affect how a person's brain processes information, learns, and behaves. It encompasses a variety of neurological differences, including: Autism spectrum disorder (ASD), Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), Dyslexia, Dyspraxia, and Tourette's syndrome.

Neurodivergence is not a medical diagnosis, but rather a term used to describe people whose brains function differently from what is considered typical or "neurotypical." It is important to note that neurodivergence is not a disability or a disorder, but simply a different way of experiencing the world.

Individuals with neurodivergence may have unique strengths and challenges, and their experiences can vary widely. Some may face difficulties with social interactions, learning, or sensory processing, while others may have exceptional abilities in certain areas.

The term "neurodivergence" was coined in the 1990s by sociologist Judy Singer to promote understanding and acceptance of neurological differences. It is a positive and empowering term that emphasizes the diversity and richness of human brains.
